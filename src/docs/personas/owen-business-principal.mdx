In small operations (under 25 employees), Owen is the shop owner who does
everything &#8212; ordering, receiving, floor work &#8212; and adopted Arda
because whiteboards and memory were failing. In medium operations (25&#8211;200
employees), Owen delegates the daily work to Irene, David, and Keisha but
retains accountability for whether the system delivers value.

**Responsibilities:** Owns the business case for Arda. Decides to adopt the
system, funds the subscription, and is accountable for ROI. Sets the strategic
direction: which inventory areas to bring under kanban management first, what
&#8220;good&#8221; looks like, and when to expand from pilot to full rollout. In
the smallest shops, also performs daily procurement (wearing David&#8217;s hat).
Success is measured in business terms: fewer stockouts, shorter lead times,
reduced emergency purchases, and team confidence.

## Goals and Motivations

**Professional Goals:**

- Never stop a job because a supply ran out. Every stockout is a direct cost:
  idle labor, missed deadlines, expedited shipping, lost customer trust.
- Build a system that compounds: every time something goes wrong, the fix is
  captured so the same mistake never happens again.
- Reduce dependence on any single person&#8217;s memory. If David is sick on
  Monday, someone else can process the queue because the knowledge lives in
  the system.
- Start fast, improve continuously. Get the system running with good-enough
  data and refine over weeks, not months.

**Motivations:**

- Seeing the team trust the system. When a worker scans a card and walks away
  confident that the item will arrive, Owen&#8217;s investment has paid off.
- Predictable supplier spend. Knowing what was ordered, from whom, at what
  price &#8212; without digging through old Amazon order confirmations or email
  threads.
- Reclaiming time spent on fire drills. Before Arda, Owen spent hours each
  week on emergency runs, phone calls to suppliers, and tracking down who
  ordered what.
- Observable operations: being able to glance at the dashboard and know the
  state of procurement without asking anyone.

## Challenges and Pain Points

- **Initial setup burden:** Uploading purchase history, categorizing items,
  setting reorder points for hundreds of items feels overwhelming. Wants to
  import from vendor order history and have the system suggest defaults.
- **Imperfect data at the start:** Reorder quantities may be wrong (case of 6
  vs. individual units), supplier URLs may be missing. Needs to accept
  imperfect data and validate later without the system blocking progress.
- **Incremental rollout:** Cannot switch the whole shop at once. Needs to start
  with one category and expand area by area.
- **Trust erosion:** If the first batch of cards has wrong quantities or missing
  supplier info, the team loses faith in the system.
- **Continuous improvement discipline:** When a wrong SKU is ordered or a
  pack-size mismatch causes an over-order, the correction must happen
  immediately and stick.
- **Physical setup knowledge gap:** Bins, dividers, card placement, and
  reorder-point positioning require physical infrastructure that software
  cannot teach. Owen needs examples, guides, and possibly videos.
- **Cultural adoption:** The team may see Arda as &#8220;more work&#8221;
  rather than &#8220;less chaos.&#8221;

**Fears and Objections:**

- The system becomes shelfware &#8212; adopted with enthusiasm but abandoned
  within months because the team found workarounds faster than learning the
  process.
- Paying for a subscription that does not measurably reduce emergency orders or
  stockouts.
- Losing institutional knowledge if the system replaces manual processes before
  it has captured enough data to be reliable.
- The team sees Arda as &#8220;more work&#8221; rather than &#8220;less
  chaos.&#8221; Cultural adoption is harder than technical adoption.

## Typical Day

1. **Morning:** Quick check on whether yesterday&#8217;s orders went out. In
   small operations, Owen processes the queue directly. In larger ones, a
   dashboard glance or status check with Irene.
2. **Mid-morning:** Handle exceptions &#8212; backorders, wrong items, quantity
   mismatches. Decide: reorder from a secondary supplier? Adjust the minimum?
3. **Throughout the day:** Notice operational pain points. &#8220;We keep
   running out of that adhesive.&#8221; &#8220;Why did we order 10 when we only
   needed 6?&#8221; Owen wants to fix these in the moment.
4. **Weekly:** Review what went wrong and what went right. Fewer emergency runs?
   Which categories are running smoothly on kanban?
5. **Monthly:** Evaluate ROI. Is the subscription justified? Should more areas
   be brought under management?

**Session pattern:** Variable. In small operations, 30&#8211;60 minutes daily
doing David&#8217;s work. In larger operations, 2&#8211;3 check-ins per week
for 5&#8211;15 minutes &#8212; dashboards, escalations, strategic decisions.

## Technology and Environment

- May use a desktop in the office and a phone on the shop floor.
- Comfortable with consumer web apps (Amazon, banking) but not enterprise
  software. Expects Arda to be as simple as placing an Amazon order.
- Low tolerance for configuration screens, modal dialogs, or multi-step wizards.
- Will use a phone to scan a QR code but prefers a full-size screen for most
  work.

## Personality

Pragmatic, impatient with complexity, results-oriented. Values systems that earn
trust through reliability, not through feature count. Willing to accept
imperfect data today if the system lets them improve it over time. Leads by
demonstrating &#8212; if Owen scans cards, the team will scan cards. Suspicious
of software that requires training before it delivers value.

## Information Sources

Owen draws on operational experience more than formal research:

- Supplier catalogs and order history: Amazon Business, Uline, Grainger,
  McMaster-Carr.
- Old emails and invoices for pricing and SKU data.
- Conversations with the team about what runs out and what does not.
- The Arda help center &#8212; but only when stuck. Owen prefers learning by
  doing over reading documentation.

## Decision-Making Process

Owen decides by gut and experience, validated by data when available. Arda was
adopted because the pain of the current state (sticky notes, memory, emergency
runs) exceeded the pain of change. Owen expands to new categories when the
current pilot is demonstrably working, delegates operational execution but
retains strategic control. If something goes wrong twice, the goal is to fix
the root cause &#8212; not apply another workaround.

## Communication Preferences

- **Urgent issues:** Prefers face-to-face or phone.
- **Day-to-day operations:** Treats Arda&#8217;s queue and dashboard as the
  shared source of truth rather than email threads.
- **System feedback:** Appreciates when the system surfaces problems (items
  without suppliers, stale quantities) rather than hiding them.
- **Notifications:** Does not want to be notified about normal operations
  &#8212; only exceptions and failures.
